10 Critical Questions to Ask Before Buying a House in Kenya (2026)

Houses can look perfect on a viewing day—but hidden issues show up after you pay. Use this 10-question checklist to confirm documents, approvals, utilities, build quality, neighborhood risks, and total costs.

Written by Fridah Kawira
8 min read
Updated: February 3, 2026
Key Takeaway

A “nice house” is not the same as a “safe purchase.” Your safest path is: independent verification + inspection + lawyer-reviewed agreement + staged payment.

Buying a home in Kenya—whether in Syokimau, Kitengela, Ngong, Athi River, Rongai, or Nairobi—requires more than a quick viewing. Below are the 10 questions I insist every buyer asks (and documents they should demand) before paying any deposit.

Why These Questions Matter

Home buying risks usually fall into three buckets: legal risk (wrong owner / disputes), physical risk (poor construction / hidden defects), and financial risk (hidden costs / overpricing). This checklist is designed to protect you from all three.

The 10 Critical Questions

1

Who is the registered owner (and can we confirm it officially)?

Don’t rely on ID copies alone. Confirm ownership using an official search and ensure the seller’s ID matches the registered owner. If it’s a company, confirm directors/authority to sell.

2

Is the title clean—any charges, restrictions, or court issues?

A house can be beautiful and still have a charge, mortgage, restriction, or dispute. Ask your advocate to confirm the status before signing.

3

Is the land use and development approved?

For estates and developments, confirm approvals and compliance (where applicable). This helps avoid buying into areas later affected by enforcement, demolitions, or planning restrictions.

4

What exactly is included in the sale?

Fixtures, fittings, water tanks, solar, in-built appliances, wardrobes, gates, cabro, CCTV—list everything clearly in writing. Avoid “tutaongea” promises.

5

Is water reliable? (Source, schedule, and cost)

Ask: water source (borehole/utility), reliability, rationing schedule, monthly cost, and if storage tanks are included. Water is a lifestyle factor—and a hidden cost.

6

Is electricity stable and safely installed?

Confirm KPLC connection (or alternative), check for safe wiring, and ask about outages. If solar exists, confirm capacity, batteries, and warranty.

7

Any structural issues—cracks, dampness, leaking, drainage?

Inspect walls, ceilings, bathrooms, roof lines, and drainage. Ask about past repairs. If possible, use a professional inspection—especially for older homes.

8

How is security in the area (day and night)?

Ask neighbors and local businesses. Confirm estate security (if gated), response time, and recent incidents. Don’t rely on “ni place poa tu.”

9

What are the running costs?

Ask for an estimate of monthly costs: service charge, garbage, security, water, electricity, and repairs. For estates/apartments, request the official service charge breakdown.

10

What is the full “all-in” cost to complete the purchase?

Price is not the full cost. Confirm extra costs like legal fees, stamp duty, valuation (if financing), consents, and transfer costs. Your advocate should give you a completion cost estimate before you commit.

Fast Red Flags

Be careful if: the seller refuses an official search, wants cash-only, avoids lawyer involvement, changes details repeatedly, or pressures you with “many buyers are waiting.”

Documents You Should Request

  • Ownership proof + official search details (independent)
  • Sale agreement (lawyer reviewed)
  • Rates / rent status (where applicable)
  • Development approvals / compliance docs (for estates/projects where relevant)
  • Utilities proof (water/electricity arrangements)
Pro Tip from Fridah

If you’re unsure, pause and verify. A good deal today is still a good deal tomorrow—if it’s genuine. Paying in a rush is how buyers lose money.

FAQs

Should I pay a deposit before verification?
Avoid paying before independent verification and a clear written agreement. If you must “reserve,” ensure it’s documented, refundable under clear conditions, and reviewed by your advocate.
What if the home is in an estate with a management company?
Ask for service charge details, estate rules, and any pending disputes. Confirm what happens if you miss a payment and what services are guaranteed.
How many viewings should I do?
At least two—ideally at different times of day. One visit should focus on the home itself; another should focus on access road, security, noise, and neighborhood.
